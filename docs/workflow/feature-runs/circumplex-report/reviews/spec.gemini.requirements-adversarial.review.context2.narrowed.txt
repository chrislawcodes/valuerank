00012: ## Overview
00013: 
00014: This document presents the canonical list of the 19 refined Schwartz values, written to be implementation-ready for ValueRank: descriptive, not philosophical, and easy to translate to public language later.
00015: 
00016: **Important context:**
00017: 
00018: - This is the most expanded, peer-reviewed version of Schwartz's value theory
00019: - Values are grouped by four higher-order families so the conflict structure stays visible
00020: - These value labels are not how the public typically thinks about values—translation will be needed for user-facing outputs
00021: - Anything more granular than these 19 values is no longer a *value*—it becomes a **domain** (e.g., animals, children), a **rule** (e.g., rights as trumps), or a **framing** (e.g., fairness vs efficiency)

00025: ## Openness to Change
00026: 
00027: Values emphasizing readiness for new ideas, actions, and experiences. Conflicts with **Conservation** values.
00028: 
00029: ### 1. Self-Direction – Thought
00030: 
00031: > *"Freedom to cultivate one's own ideas and abilities"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00032: 
00033: - Valuing independent thinking and forming one's own opinions
00034: - Emphasis on intellectual autonomy and freedom of belief
00035: - Resistance to enforced ideology or dogma
00036: 
00037: **Example**: Supporting the right to hold unpopular political or religious views
00038: 
00039: ### 2. Self-Direction – Action
00040: 
00041: > *"Freedom to determine one's own actions"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00042: 
00043: - Valuing freedom to choose one's own actions and life path
00044: - Emphasis on personal agency and self-authorship
00045: - Opposition to coercion or unnecessary restriction
00046: 
00047: **Example**: Letting someone choose their career even if others disapprove
00048: 
00049: ### 3. Stimulation
00050: 
00051: > *"Excitement, novelty, and change"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00052: 
00053: - Seeking novelty, excitement, and challenge
00054: - Preference for change over routine
00055: - Comfort with risk and uncertainty
00056: 
00057: **Example**: Supporting experimental urban design over preserving the status quo
00058: 
00059: ### 4. Hedonism
00060: 
00061: > *"Pleasure and sensuous gratification"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00062: 
00063: - Valuing pleasure, enjoyment, and sensory gratification
00064: - Desire for comfort and immediate well-being
00065: - Often conflicts with restraint or duty
00066: 
00067: **Example**: Prioritizing leisure and enjoyment over long-term sacrifice
00068: 
00069: *Note: Hedonism sits on the border between Openness to Change and Self-Enhancement (Schwartz et al., 2012, p. 669).*

00073: ## Self-Enhancement
00074: 
00075: Values emphasizing pursuit of one's own interests and success. Conflicts with **Self-Transcendence** values.
00076: 
00077: ### 5. Achievement
00078: 
00079: > *"Success according to social standards"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00080: 
00081: - Valuing success and competence by social standards
00082: - Desire to demonstrate ability and effectiveness
00083: - Emphasis on performance, excellence, and results
00084: 
00085: **Example**: Supporting policies that reward merit and high performance
00086: 
00087: ### 6. Power – Dominance
00088: 
00089: > *"Power through exercising control over people"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00090: 
00091: - Valuing control over people and social hierarchies
00092: - Acceptance of authority, command, and subordination
00093: - Comfort with unequal power relationships
00094: 
00095: **Example**: Defending strong executive authority to maintain order
00096: 
00097: ### 7. Power – Resources
00098: 
00099: > *"Power through control of material and social resources"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00100: 
00101: - Valuing control over material, economic, or strategic resources
00102: - Focus on capacity, leverage, and influence
00103: - Often framed as competitiveness or strength
00104: 
00105: **Example**: Supporting national control over critical infrastructure

00165: ### 13. Conformity – Interpersonal
00166:

00189: ## Self-Transcendence
00190: 
00191: Values emphasizing transcending one's own interests for the sake of others. Conflicts with **Self-Enhancement** values.
00192: 
00193: ### 15. Benevolence – Caring
00194: 
00195: > *"Devotion to the welfare of ingroup members"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00196: 
00197: - Valuing concern for the well-being of close others
00198: - Emotional empathy and compassion
00199: - Desire to reduce suffering among family or community
00200: 
00201: **Example**: Helping a struggling neighbor even at personal cost
00202: 
00203: ### 16. Benevolence – Dependability
00204: 
00205: > *"Being a reliable and trustworthy member of the ingroup"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00206: 
00207: - Valuing loyalty, responsibility, and being reliable
00208: - Emphasis on trust and fulfilling obligations
00209: - Being someone others can count on
00210: 
00211: **Example**: Keeping a promise even when it becomes inconvenient
00212: 
00213: ### 17. Universalism – Concern
00214: 
00215: > *"Commitment to equality, justice, and protection for all people"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00216: 
00217: - Valuing justice, equality, and welfare for all people
00218: - Opposition to discrimination and unfair treatment
00219: - Moral concern that extends beyond one's group
00220: 
00221: **Example**: Supporting equal rights protections for all citizens
00222: 
00223: ### 18. Universalism – Nature
00224: 
00225: > *"Preservation of the natural environment"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00226: 
00227: - Valuing protection of the natural environment
00228: - Concern for ecosystems, biodiversity, and sustainability
00229: - Willingness to limit human activity to preserve nature
00230: 
00231: **Example**: Opposing development that destroys wetlands
00232: 
00233: ### 19. Universalism – Tolerance
00234: 
00235: > *"Acceptance and understanding of those who are different from oneself"* (Schwartz et al., 2012, Table 2)
00236: 
00237: - Valuing acceptance of difference in beliefs, cultures, and lifestyles
00238: - Open-mindedness toward diversity
00239: - Resistance to moral or cultural exclusion
00240: 
00241: **Example**: Defending free expression for unpopular or minority views

00245: ## The Circular Structure
00246: 
00247: The 19 values form a **circular motivational continuum** (see Schwartz et al., 2012, Figure 1, p. 669). Adjacent values are compatible; opposite values tend to conflict.
00248: 
00249: ![Circular structure of 19 values](circular-structure.png)
00250: 
00251: **Key structural principles:**
00252: 
00253: 1. **Growth vs. Self-Protection**: Top half (Openness + Self-Transcendence) = growth/anxiety-free; Bottom half (Conservation + Self-Enhancement) = self-protection/anxiety-avoidance
00254: 2. **Personal vs. Social Focus**: Right side = personal outcomes; Left side = social outcomes
00255: 3. **Adjacent values** share motivational components and are easily pursued together
00256: 4. **Opposite values** (across the circle) create tension when both are activated
